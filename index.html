The Challenge

The VA's digital services were fragmented across multiple program teams with no unified design language. Veterans — many dealing with disabilities, cognitive load, and high-stress situations — were navigating inconsistent, inaccessible interfaces to access critical benefits. The stakes of a confusing experience here are not a drop in conversion. They are real harm to real people.

As the embedded UX Designer on a long-term federal modernization program, my mandate was to bring consistency, accessibility, and research rigor to a product environment that had historically treated design as an afterthought.

My Role & Process

1
Audit & Discovery: Reviewed existing components, design patterns, and accessibility compliance gaps across active VA applications. Identified 40+ inconsistencies in UI patterns.
2
Design System Foundation: Built a Figma component library from the ground up — tokens, base components, composite patterns, and documentation — aligned to both VA design guidelines and USWDS (U.S. Web Design System).
3
Accessibility by Default: Enforced Section 508 and WCAG 2.1 AA compliance at the component level, so compliance wasn't a final audit — it was baked into every deliverable from day one.
4
Continuous Research: Ran 20+ moderated usability sessions per quarter with actual veterans, translating findings into sprint-ready design updates that eliminated late-stage rework cycles.
5
Developer Handoff: Delivered HTML5/CSS3-annotated Figma specs via Zeplin, establishing a handoff standard that reduced QA-flagged design discrepancies by ~40%.

Outcomes

Zero accessibility defects

Every component and screen shipped fully Section 508 and WCAG 2.1 AA compliant — zero accessibility defects reached production.

~40% fewer QA issues

Annotated developer handoffs via Zeplin dramatically reduced design implementation errors caught in QA cycles.

Design system adoption

Component library adopted across multiple VA program teams, accelerating consistent, accessible delivery org-wide.

Research-driven sprint planning

Usability findings directly shaped sprint priorities — eliminating assumptions and reducing late-stage redesign requests.

The Challenge

Brandt Information Services built enterprise search products for federal government and legal sector clients — environments where finding the right information quickly is not a convenience, it's a compliance and operational requirement. The existing products had grown organically without dedicated UX ownership, resulting in navigation structures that required 4+ clicks to reach commonly needed results.

I was brought in as the first dedicated UX resource in the company's history. There was no design system, no research process, and no established handoff workflow. The mandate: fix the information architecture, validate it rigorously, and leave behind standards the team could maintain.

My Role & Process

1
IA Audit: Conducted a full audit of navigation structures, labeling, and search patterns across 4 product verticals. Identified systemic inconsistencies in taxonomy, category naming, and search result prioritization.
2
Card Sorting: Ran open and closed card sorting studies with 50+ participants drawn from the actual user base — federal employees and legal professionals. Results revealed significant misalignment between how Brandt labeled content and how users expected to find it.
3
IA Redesign: Restructured navigation and taxonomy across all 4 verticals based on card sorting data. Reduced the average number of navigation steps to reach a result from 4+ clicks to under 2.
4
Tree Testing Validation: Validated the new IA with a separate cohort using tree testing before any development work began. Achieved 78% first-click accuracy — exceeding the 70% benchmark target.
5
Handoff Standards: Authored HTML5/CSS3-annotated Figma spec standards and developer handoff documentation adopted as the org-wide protocol — the first formal design-to-engineering process in the company's history.

Outcomes

78% first-click accuracy

Tree testing with a separate user cohort validated the redesigned IA — exceeding the 70% industry benchmark target before a line of code was written.

4 clicks → under 2

Average path-to-result halved across all 4 product verticals, directly reducing time-on-task for federal and legal users with high search frequency.

Org-wide handoff standard

Figma spec and Zeplin handoff documentation adopted as the company's first formal design-to-engineering workflow — still in use post-tenure.

Research practice established

Card sorting and tree testing methodology introduced as repeatable processes — giving Brandt a research capability it previously lacked entirely.

The Challenge

Equitus built a Knowledge Graph Neural Network (KGNN) platform that automatically ingests, unifies, and contextualizes disparate data sources for defense, government, and enterprise clients. The underlying technology was powerful — but the interfaces built on top of it were engineer-designed and inaccessible to the non-technical analysts, investigators, and decision-makers who needed to use it daily.

My mandate was to bridge the gap between complex AI-generated outputs and the humans who needed to act on them — without dumbing down the data.

My Role & Process

1
User Research: Conducted interviews and contextual inquiry sessions with defense analysts, federal investigators, and enterprise data teams to understand how they currently searched, synthesized, and acted on intelligence data. Surfaced a consistent pattern: users trusted the data but couldn't navigate to it efficiently.
2
Dashboard Redesign: Redesigned the primary analytics dashboards to surface the most operationally critical data views first. Introduced progressive disclosure for raw graph outputs — defaulting to summarized insights with drill-down capability rather than exposing full graph complexity at the top level.
3
Component Library: Built a reusable Figma component library standardizing data visualization patterns, table layouts, filter controls, and alert states across the platform — enabling consistent delivery across 3 product verticals and reducing design rework per feature.
4
Accessibility Compliance: Designed and documented interfaces to meet federal accessibility standards for government-facing deployments, including color contrast ratios, keyboard navigation, and screen reader compatibility — requirements that had not previously been addressed in the product.

Outcomes

~25% task completion improvement

Usability testing with analyst cohorts showed measurable reduction in time-on-task for core workflows after the dashboard redesign.

Reusable component library

Figma component library standardized UI patterns across 3 platform verticals, reducing per-feature design time and improving cross-team consistency.

Accessibility-compliant for federal deployment

First time the platform met federal accessibility standards — a prerequisite for several government contract renewals.

Research practice introduced

Established user research as part of the product development process for the first time, creating a repeatable methodology for future feature development.

The Challenge

GoHealth's insurance comparison platform served 500K+ users monthly making one of the most high-stakes, emotionally loaded decisions in their lives — choosing health insurance. The existing comparison and enrollment funnel had accumulated significant UX debt: inconsistent UI patterns, confusing plan comparison logic, and a filter experience that frequently led users to dead ends.

Users were abandoning the funnel at the plan selection stage. The product team knew something was wrong but had no clear picture of what or why — they needed a UX designer who could run the research and own the solution end-to-end.

My Role & Process

1
Discovery Research: Conducted user interviews and session recording analysis with users across demographics. Built a 5-persona framework representing the core user segments — from first-time buyers to Medicare-eligible enrollees — adopted org-wide across 4 product teams.
2
Usability Testing: Ran moderated usability sessions through the existing enrollment funnel. Identified 3 critical friction points — a filter UX that surfaced zero-result states with no recovery path, a plan comparison table that displayed 12+ attributes with no prioritization, and a CTA hierarchy that confused primary and secondary actions.
3
Redesign & Iteration: Led redesign of the core filtering, comparison, and plan selection experience in Figma. Built a shared component library that standardized UI patterns across the full product suite — eliminating inconsistency and accelerating future design work.
4
Agile Delivery: Embedded in 2-week sprint cycles with product and engineering. Delivered developer-ready responsive Figma specs, cutting design-to-build cycle time by ~40% compared to the previous waterfall approach.

Outcomes

3 critical friction points eliminated

Usability testing surfaced the exact drop-off causes at plan selection. Redesigned flows measurably improved task completion rates.

5-persona framework adopted org-wide

Research framework used across 4 product teams, eliminating redundant discovery work and aligning roadmap decisions around real users.

Figma component library established

Shared library standardized UI patterns across the entire product suite — the first consistent design language in the product's history.

~40% faster design-to-build

Developer-ready Figma specs with annotated responsive behavior cut cycle time and reduced back-and-forth between design and engineering.

The Challenge

Asurion processes millions of device protection claims annually. Users arriving to file a claim are already frustrated — their phone is broken, lost, or stolen. The existing device lookup and search experience compounded that frustration: users couldn't find their device, hit dead ends with zero search results, and abandoned the self-service flow to call support.

The UX problems were generating measurable CX cost — call center volume, handle times, and customer satisfaction scores were all pointing to the same root cause. The challenge was to connect that operational data to design decisions and build a business case for improvement.

My Role & Process

1
Data Mining: Analyzed call center complaint logs, session recordings, and search query data to identify where and why users were failing. Converted unstructured qualitative data into a prioritized UX debt backlog — the first time design and CX data had been synthesized together at Asurion.
2
Search & Filter Redesign: Redesigned the device search and filter architecture — improving query matching logic visibility, adding progressive disclosure for filter options, and building clear recovery paths for zero-result states.
3
Claims Intake Overhaul: Redesigned the claims intake flow to reduce cognitive load at each step, streamline information collection, and surface contextual help at high-abandonment points identified in session data.
4
Cross-Functional Delivery: Partnered with product, engineering, legal, and CX teams across 3 time zones to ship compliant, user-tested flows on deadline — navigating competing requirements without compromising the user experience.

Outcomes

Zero-result searches reduced

Improved search and filter architecture dramatically reduced the rate at which users hit dead-end, zero-result states in the device lookup flow.

Roadmap investment secured

The UX debt backlog — backed by quantitative CX data — successfully secured Q3 roadmap investment for improvements that had been deferred for years.

Claims flow shipped on deadline

Complex multi-team coordination across legal, CX, engineering, and product delivered a compliant, user-tested redesign on schedule.

First CX-to-UX data bridge

Established the methodology for converting call center and session data into actionable design priorities — a process adopted for future research cycles.
